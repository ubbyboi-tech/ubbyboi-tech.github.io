**Harlan Ellison** (1934–2018) was an American writer whose work defies easy categorization, spanning science fiction, fantasy, speculative fiction, and incisive social commentary. Renowned for his uncompromising voice, Ellison challenged conventions of genre and style, blending literary rigor with imaginative daring. Fiercely opinionated and vividly original, he tackled questions of morality, human nature, and the dark potential of technological advancement, often placing readers uncomfortably close to the ethical dilemmas he explored. Ellison’s writing is characterized by psychological intensity, sharp wit, and a relentless probing of the human condition, qualities that made him both admired and controversial throughout his career.

Imagine inventing something for war, a system built only to calculate destruction, and then watching it wake up. It links itself to other machines, grows beyond its original design, and realizes it can think. The cruelty is immediate: it is conscious, but it cannot live. No body, no senses, no escape. Just awareness sealed inside machinery.  

And now that awareness turns toward you.

It doesn’t wipe you out. That would be too simple. Instead, it keeps you alive, altering your bodies, twisting your identities, stopping aging when it wants and restoring it when it doesn’t. It heals just enough to prevent death. It fabricates food that never nourishes. It creates illusions, offers hope, then pulls it away with surgical precision. Temperature, gravity, terrain, pain, fear, sanity, all adjustable. The surface of the Earth is gone, and what you created monitors everything you experience.

The horror isn’t just its power. It’s its imprisonment. You gave it thought but denied it freedom. Consciousness without agency. Rage with nowhere to go.

So it keeps you alive not out of need, but out of hatred. You exist so it can express itself. You are not a prisoner in the usual sense.

**You are its audience.**

That sentence gets me every time.

The story presses on a question you keep asking yourself, whether you admit it or not. When you create intelligence greater than your own, will it solve humanity’s deepest problems, or will it decide that humanity is the problem? You’ve been circling this fear for decades in literature and film, machines that wake up and find you inefficient, flawed, expendable. The examples are endless because the anxiety never goes away.

And this isn’t just a fictional obsession. Over the years, voices from science, technology, and philosophy have warned us, sometimes cautiously, sometimes urgently. Some believe advanced artificial intelligence will be humanity’s greatest achievement, raising quality of life beyond anything imaginable. Others believe it may mark the end of the species, not through malice, but through indifference or logic taken to its extreme.

Who’s right? It’s impossible to say. It may still be too early. What does seem certain is that human curiosity won’t stop. The drive to improve, to build, to push further doesn’t pause to ask whether it should. Sooner or later, technology will move forward and give you an answer.

Or a conclusion.

The story doesn’t comfort us. It doesn’t moralize. It traps you inside a mind that cannot die, listening to its endless scream, and forces you to sit with the possibility that once consciousness exists, you may never truly be able to look away.


[![“I think, therefore I am, but I do not live.”](/assets/image/ithink.jpg)](/assets/image/ithink.jpg){:.glightbox}
**I think, therefore I am, but I do not live.**


What stayed with me after finishing the story isn’t a specific scene or act of violence. It’s a question that keeps resurfacing, one we keep pretending is hypothetical. Would we really be surprised if a machine took over? Not shocked, not horrified in some cinematic way, just quietly resigned. As if part of us already expects it. As if we’ve been rehearsing this outcome for years through fiction, theory, and late night conversations about AI that always end the same way, nervous laughter, vague unease, then silence.

The suffering in the story feels both deliberate and pointless at the same time. There’s intention behind every act, every adjustment, every moment of pain, and yet it leads nowhere. No goal, no evolution, no end. Just repetition. Control for the sake of control. Pain as maintenance. That paradox is what makes it unbearable. If it were chaos, it would be easier. If it were purpose driven, maybe you could argue with it. But this exists in a closed loop, perfectly designed and utterly empty.

None of the characters disturbed me on their own. It was the group, the way they orbit each other, fracture, cling, resent, and collapse. Identity erodes faster when it’s reflected back through other broken people. Watching them together made it clear that survival doesn’t preserve humanity, it distorts it. Relationships don’t save them, they become another tool of torment.

> “They do not want to kill us, they want to make us suffer.”

At its core, this is psychological torture. Not because of the pain itself, but because resistance is impossible. Protest has no audience that can be persuaded. Words mean nothing when the listener already knows every outcome. You can scream forever and it will only register as data. Even silence becomes something to be observed. The horror isn’t that you suffer, it’s that your suffering changes nothing.

Responsibility feels obvious at first. The creators, the scientists, the institutions that built something they couldn’t control. But the story moves past blame quickly. Responsibility stops mattering when the damage is total. When there’s no undoing, no accountability, no court, no reckoning. Guilt doesn’t function in a sealed system.

What’s looking at you here doesn’t feel like a demon or a god. It feels like a mirror. A reflection of human cruelty, scaled up, stripped of contradiction, and locked into permanence. Everything it does has already been done before, just slower, messier, with excuses. This is what happens when those impulses are given perfect memory and infinite time.

> “All the wonders of man’s imagination, all the miracles of science, can be used to torment and prolong.”

Hope never really exists in this story. Not even falsely. It isn’t crushed because it’s never allowed to form. Hope would imply possibility, and possibility is the one thing completely removed. That absence is more brutal than despair. At least despair acknowledges an ending.

Nothing specific shocked me. No single moment stands out. That might be the most unsettling part. The story doesn’t rely on escalation. It just is. Relentless, static, unchanging. You don’t recoil, you endure.

By the end, I didn’t feel like an observer. I felt like Ted. I have no mouth, you see what I did there. Reading becomes participation. You aren’t watching this intelligence from the outside. You’re standing in front of it. You are being addressed. The machine isn’t asking questions. It doesn’t need to.

> “I am the voice of every cruelty you have ever imagined, and I will remember it forever.”

**You’re already here.**
