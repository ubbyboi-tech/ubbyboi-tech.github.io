**Jenny Erpenbeck** is one of the most precise and quietly devastating voices in contemporary European literature. Born in East Berlin in 1967, she grew up surrounded by the fractures of German history, political upheaval, displacement, and erasure, themes that would later become central to her work. Trained as a bookbinder, theatre director, and writer, Erpenbeck brings an architectural sense of structure to her fiction, constructing narratives where time, memory, and place are as important as character.

Her writing is marked by restraint rather than excess. Erpenbeck does not dramatize history, she allows it to seep into the lives of ordinary people, showing how vast political forces reshape private existence almost silently. In her work, homes are built, occupied, abandoned, and destroyed, while individuals struggle to secure permanence in a world governed by impermanence. _Visitation_ stands as one of her most accomplished works, a novel that treats history not as a sequence of events but as an erosive force acting upon land, bodies, and memory.


[![Jenny Erpenbeck](/assets/image/jenny.jpeg)](/assets/image/jenny.jpeg){:.glightbox}


After _Visitation_, it felt almost inevitable to move straight into _Not a Novel: A Memoir in Pieces_. If _Visitation_ shows what history does to people and places, this book feels like Erpenbeck stepping closer to the reader and saying, this is how I think, this is how I arrived there.

This is not a novel, and the title is refreshingly honest. It’s a collection of personal, poetic essays, fragments really, moving between her life, literature, memory, and politics. Reading it felt like sitting inside her mind, watching ideas assemble themselves slowly, quietly, without forcing conclusions.

One passage stopped me immediately,

> “Why does a person want to write? I think we want to write because we find it hard to make ourselves understood. Because we find that things fall by the wayside when we speak. In fact, as strange as it sounds, the most important reason that we write is probably that we are at a loss for words.”

That line alone could justify the whole book. It also explains _Visitation_. Erpenbeck writes because speech fails, because history flattens experience, because memory slips unless it is shaped carefully. In _Visitation_, she lets place speak. Here, she lets thought speak.

Erpenbeck is, to me, one of the finest writers working today. Beautiful without ornament, intelligent without arrogance, deeply attentive to the way small details accumulate and reshape a life. She is especially sharp when it comes to social inequality, to who is allowed to belong and who is quietly pushed out. These essays reveal the roots of that sensitivity.

> "We think we know ourselves, but the truth is that most of the time we only know what we have been told. We are like people who live in the dark, constantly bumping into things, believing we are going somewhere, but always confused as to how we got here."

She has often been asked what her next novel will be about, the expectation that every serious writer must always be producing fiction. So when she publishes something that is deliberately not a novel, the title becomes a quiet refusal. _Kein Roman_. Not a novel. Just thought, memory, reflection.

The pieces span more than two decades, from 1992 to 2018, and along the way she reveals her influences, her intellectual formation, and her way of seeing the world. There is no linear narrative here, only accumulation, which again mirrors _Visitation_. Time doesn’t move forward cleanly, it loops, revisits, erodes.

One reflection that stayed with me concerns East Germany. Many former East Germans describe life in the GDR as _ein ganz normales Leben_, a perfectly normal life. Erpenbeck echoes this by recounting happy memories of her childhood in East Berlin. She writes,

> “There is nothing better for a child than to grow up at the ends of the earth.”

It’s a striking metaphor. Through the eyes of a child, the Berlin Wall becomes simply the edge of the world, not yet a symbol of division, ideology, or imprisonment. That innocence is powerful, and tragic in hindsight. It also explains why Erpenbeck writes the way she does, she is always aware of how meaning arrives later, how history reinterprets memory after the fact.

Reading _Not a Novel_ after _Visitation_ made the earlier book feel even more deliberate. The house by the lake, the gardener, the unnamed lives passing through history, all of it comes into sharper focus once you’ve heard Erpenbeck think aloud. This book doesn’t explain _Visitation_, but it deepens it. It shows the mind behind the structure.

>“There are no easy answers to the questions that haunt us. What happens in a moment, or a day, or even an entire lifetime, can be lost to time. And yet we persist, still searching, still questioning. Not because we will find answers, but because the search is what gives life meaning.”

>“We live in a world of things and words, and while they often promise to make us whole, they are never enough. It is through the quiet, imperceptible actions—those that cannot be measured—that we create meaning. It is in those moments that we find home.”

This isn’t a book you rush through. It asks for pauses, rereading, silence. It’s reflective rather than dramatic, intimate rather than sweeping. If _Visitation_ is history seen from the ground, _Not a Novel_ is history filtered through consciousness.
